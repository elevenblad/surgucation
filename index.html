Wilms Tumor: Epidemiology

NCCN v2.2025
  • 90%+ of renal tumors in patients <20 years
  • Incidence: 5.7 per million children
  • ~500–600 new US cases annually
  • 75%+ present between ages 1–5 years

Molecular Biology of Wilms Tumor

Key Genetic Loci

WT1 (11p13)
Tumor suppressor gene; mutated in WAGR and Denys-Drash. Involved in kidney/gonad development.
WT2 / 11p15 (IGF2)
Imprinting center; LOH or LOI at 11p15 associated with Beckwith-Wiedemann. IGF2 overexpression.
CTNNB1 (β-catenin)
Wnt pathway activating mutations; often co-occurs with WT1 mutations.
WTX (AMER1) at Xq11.1
Deleted/mutated in ~30% of WT; Wnt pathway regulator.

Prognostic Biomarkers

Unfavorable Markers:
  • 1q gain — Most powerful adverse marker
  • Combined LOH 1p + 16q — Increased relapse risk
  • 11p15 LOH/LOI — Fewer data, under investigation
Clinical Impact:
  • Used to intensify treatment in Stage I/II FHWT
  • COG AREN trials: 1q gain + LOH 1p/16q → upgrade to DD4A or Regimen M
  • SIOP: Blastemal type after chemo = high risk
Key: TP53 mutations are hallmark of anaplastic histology and predict chemoresistance.

Diagnostic Imaging

Initial Workup

  • Abdominal ultrasound — first-line
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast or MRI
  • Chest CT — lung metastasis screening
  • Urine catecholamines (HVA/VMA) — rule out neuroblastoma

Lab Studies

  • CBC with differential
  • CMP, urinalysis
  • Coagulation studies (PT/PTT)
  • Screen for predisposition syndromes

Imaging Characteristics

Wilms Tumor:
  • Large, well-circumscribed intrarenal mass
  • Heterogeneous enhancement
  • "Claw sign" — normal renal parenchyma wrapping around tumor
  • Minimal calcification (5–10%)
  • Check for renal vein/IVC tumor thrombus
CMN on MRI:
  • Classic: Homogeneous, "concentric ring sign" (58%)
  • T2-isointense, T1-hypointense
  • Cellular type: more heterogeneous, larger

COG vs. SIOP: Two Philosophies

COG (North America)

  • Upfront surgery → then chemotherapy
  • Staging based on surgical/pathologic findings
  • Allows complete histologic assessment
  • Identifies anaplasia pre-chemo
  • Risk: more surgical complications (14.6% tumor rupture)
VS

SIOP (Europe/International)

  • Neoadjuvant chemotherapy → then surgery
  • Staging after preoperative treatment
  • Tumor shrinkage → easier resection
  • 0% tumor rupture rate
  • Risk: may alter histology, miss anaplasia
Bottom Line: Both approaches yield comparable overall survival (>90% for favorable histology). The debate continues, but outcomes are equally excellent.

Histologic Classification

Favorable Histology (FHWT)

  • Triphasic pattern: blastemal + epithelial + stromal
  • Can be biphasic or monophasic
  • No anaplastic features
  • 5-year OS: >90%
SIOP Risk Groups (post-chemo):
Low Completely necrotic
Intermediate Epithelial, stromal, mixed, regressive
High Blastemal predominant, diffuse anaplasia

Anaplastic Histology

Focal Anaplasia:
  • Defined focus within primary tumor
  • Confined to renal parenchyma
  • 1–2 foci, <15mm each
  • Not in vascular spaces
  • Better prognosis with appropriate therapy
Diffuse Anaplasia:
  • Multifocal / non-localized
  • Beyond tumor capsule
  • In vessels, extrarenal sites, or metastases
  • Stage IV: 29–45% 5-year OS
  • Associated with TP53 mutations

Doxorubicin: Cumulative dose in DD4A = 150 mg/m². Monitor echocardiogram — younger children and females more susceptible to cardiotoxicity.

Radiation Therapy

Indications

  • Stage III FHWT: Flank or whole-abdomen radiation
  • Stage IV: Whole-lung radiation for pulmonary metastases
  • Anaplastic tumors: Higher doses for Stage III DA
Timing: RT to primary site typically 10–14 days post-surgery. Coordinate with chemotherapy to avoid co-administration with full-dose dactinomycin or doxorubicin.

NOT Indicated

  • Stage I–II Favorable Histology
  • Stage I Anaplastic (focal)
  • Low-risk completely necrotic tumors
Goal of modern protocols: de-escalate radiation whenever possible to minimize late effects (second malignancies, growth impairment, cardiac toxicity).

Surgical Management

Radical Nephrectomy

1. Transabdominal approach
Preferred over flank (OR 5.3× complication risk with flank incision)
2. Explore & biopsy
Liver, peritoneal surfaces, contralateral kidney
3. Assess vessels
Evaluate renal vein/IVC for tumor thrombus
4. Lymph node sampling
Minimum 5 nodes from hilum, pericaval, para-aortic
5. En bloc nephrectomy
Avoid tumor spillage. Adrenalectomy NOT required if uninvolved.

Nephron-Sparing Surgery

Indications for NSS:
  • Bilateral tumors (Stage V)
  • Solitary kidney
  • Predisposing syndromes
  • High risk for CKD
Favorable Imaging for NSS:
  • Peripheral/polar location
  • Preserves ≥1/3 normal kidney
  • No hilar/sinus invasion
  • Clear tumor–parenchyma interface
NSS protocol: Neoadjuvant chemo 6–12 weeks → re-image at week 6 → surgery within 12 weeks of starting chemo.

Surgical Complications

Overall rate: 12.7% (down from 19.8% in NWTS-3)

Risk Factors

Major Risk Factors:
  • General surgeon performing nephrectomy: OR 9.0× vs pediatric surgeon
  • IVC extension: OR 3.8
  • Flank/paramedian incision: OR 5.3
  • Tumor ≥10 cm: OR 2.0
Preoperative chemo reduces surgical complications — 0% tumor rupture vs 14.6% with immediate nephrectomy.

Treatment

Surgery is curative: Complete nephrectomy is usually sufficient. 96–100% survival.
Chemo considerations:
  • NOT routine for classic CMN
  • Consider for cellular variant in patients >3 months
  • Tumor spillage / positive margins
  • Stage II–III cellular or mixed
  • Recurrent or metastatic disease
New Targeted Therapy: ETV6-NTRK3 fusion makes cellular CMN susceptible to TRK inhibitors (larotrectinib, entrectinib) for advanced/recurrent cases.

Pediatric Renal Cell Carcinoma

Key Differences from Adult RCC

  • 2–6% of pediatric renal tumors
  • Mean age: 9–15 years
  • Incidence increasing: +3.7%/year in children

Rare but Critical Tumors

Clear Cell Sarcoma (CCSK)

  • BCOR internal tandem duplications (85% of cases)
  • Known as the "bone-metastasizing renal tumor"
  • 23% develop bone metastases (spine, pelvis, ribs)
  • Must get bone scan (unlike Wilms)
  • Requires more intensive chemo + radiation
  • Imaging can mimic Wilms — differentiation requires pathology

Rhabdoid Tumor (MRTK)

  • SMARCB1/INI1 loss (chromosome 22q11.2)
  • Median age: <1 year
  • Most aggressive pediatric renal tumor
  • ~25% have synchronous brain tumors (AT/RT)
  • Must get brain MRI at diagnosis
  • Poor prognosis despite aggressive multimodal therapy
Critical Teaching Point: Any renal tumor in an infant <6 months with aggressive features → think rhabdoid. Any renal tumor with bone mets → think clear cell sarcoma. Both require specific additional imaging and more aggressive protocols than standard Wilms.

Nephrogenic Rests

Precursor lesions found in ~1% of infant autopsies and 35–40% of Wilms tumor specimens

Perilobar Rests (PLNR)

  • Located at kidney periphery
  • Associated with Beckwith-Wiedemann
  • 11p15 LOH/LOI
  • Tend to be multifocal → nephroblastomatosis
  • Higher risk of bilateral/metachronous WT

Intralobar Rests (ILNR)

  • Located anywhere within renal lobe
  • Associated with WAGR, Denys-Drash
  • WT1 mutations
  • Often solitary
  • Earlier age of WT development
Management: Nephroblastomatosis (diffuse/multifocal rests) → chemotherapy to prevent malignant transformation. Close surveillance with serial imaging for isolated rests.

Key Takeaways

1. Wilms tumor is the most common pediatric renal tumor (>90%). Most present as healthy children with painless abdominal mass between ages 1–5.
2. Age is your first filter: neonates → CMN, toddlers → Wilms, adolescents → RCC. Bone mets → CCSK. Brain + kidney tumor in infant → MRTK.
3. COG and SIOP represent equally valid approaches with comparable survival. Know both frameworks.
4. Molecular markers (1q gain, LOH 1p/16q, TP53) now drive treatment intensity decisions beyond stage and histology alone.
5. General surgeons performing nephrectomy = 9x complication rate. These cases require pediatric surgical expertise. Minimum 5 lymph nodes sampled.
6. Long-term survivor care is critical: 75% of excess deaths at 30–50 years from second cancers and cardiac disease.
